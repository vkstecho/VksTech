🌱 I Used to Hate Plastic. Then I Joined the Industry That Saves Food.

📖 12-min read  |  A Personal Reckoning with Hard Numbers  |  Published May 2026  |  Bookmark for the next debate at the dinner table

For the first 22 years of my life, I was certain about one thing: plastic was the problem. Polyethylene bags clogging Yamuna in Delhi, multi-layer wrappers blowing across the hills near my college in Uttarakhand, the sea of foil and film at every roadside dhaba — to me, all of it pointed to one verdict. The packaging industry was the villain. The most ethical career, for someone with an M.Tech, was anything *except* this.

Then in 2017 I joined a flexible packaging plant. Nine years later, I had to admit I had the story backwards — but not the way you might expect. The villain isn't the *material*. It's the *behavior* around the material — the free polythene culture, the fresh bag every shopping trip, the "vendor will give one anyway" mindset. The material itself is one of the highest-leverage food-security inventions of the past century. The way Indians use it day-to-day is exactly the disposal disaster I was angry about. Both are true. The public conversation almost always conflates them. This blog is the math I wish someone had shown my 22-year-old self.

📚 The Old Me — College, Conviction, Naivety

I did my B.Tech at NIT Uttarakhand. The campus sits in the lower Himalayas, and on trekking weekends my friends and I would walk across slopes that, every few hundred metres, had a dead patch of plastic — milk pouches, gutka wrappers, polythene bags from the local kirana — caught in the undergrowth. We were 19. We had read just enough Naomi Klein to feel the obvious thing: this had to stop. The companies making this stuff were the problem. The engineers building those machines were the problem.

My cousin Ankit, now my closest collaborator on rithora.com and on the technical side of vksTech, was even more committed than I was. He cared deeply about environmental issues — he would forward me reports about microplastics in fish, about kids in Mumbai's Dharavi sorting plastic with their bare hands, about the Great Pacific Garbage Patch. We argued for hours about whether *any* career in any plastic-adjacent industry could be ethical. I half-decided I would never take a job in flexible packaging.

Then real life arrived. I cleared GATE, did my M.Tech, and the job market handed me what it handed me. The first serious offer was from a flexible packaging plant in Noida — UFlex. I took it. I told myself it would be temporary. That was nine years ago.

🔍 The Day I Realised I Had the Story Backwards

The shift began about six months in, on an evening shift at the metalliser. The slitter was running a 12-micron metallised PET roll for a snack-food customer. Reels stacked up, each about 30 kg of finished film. I started doing arithmetic.

One 30-kg roll would package roughly 200,000 individual snack sachets. Each sachet weighed about 1.5g of film and held 30g of namkeen — about 1g of film per 20g of food. That food was going from a single factory in northern India to villages in seven states, places where the nearest big shop was 40 km away on a road that washed out twice a year. Without that 1.5g of film, the namkeen would never reach those villages — or it would arrive spoiled.

The thought that genuinely stopped me was about my grandmother in Bihar. She used to send me jars of homemade pickle. Half arrived broken; the rest I had to finish in two weeks. Now my mother sends me the same pickle in a 100g pouch with an inner foil layer. It lasts six months on a Delhi kitchen shelf. Same family, same recipe, twenty years apart. The polyethylene was not the problem. The polyethylene was the reason my grandmother's recipe still reaches me.

I went home that night and started reading scientific papers. The numbers were so far from what I had believed that I had to read them three times. I wasn't slightly wrong. I was wrong by an order of magnitude.

📊 The Honest Numbers — What Polyethylene Actually Saves

Three numbers anchor everything. They are well-cited and explain why my opinion shifted.

One — leverage on a single cucumber. Dhall et al. tested fresh cucumbers shrink-wrapped in 60-gauge polyethylene vs unwrapped. At ambient Indian conditions (29-33°C), unwrapped lasted 2 days, wrapped 5 days. Refrigerated: 9 days vs 15. Film weight: 1.5g. Cucumber: 250g. Source: Dhall et al., 2012. Repeated across hundreds of fruits and vegetables: plastic film extends shelf life by 2-3×. In a real Indian supply chain without continuous cold chain, that's the difference between food eaten and food binned.

Two — country-scale comparison. The FAO estimates global food waste has an annual carbon footprint of 3.3 GT CO2e. If food waste were a country, it would be Earth's third-largest emitter, behind only the US and China. Source: FAO Food Wastage Footprint. For comparison: total global plastic production and disposal in 2019 generated approximately 1.8 GT CO2e. Food waste is nearly twice plastic emissions — and the agriculture that grew the wasted food consumed 30% of the world's agricultural land.

Three — India. ICAR data for FY 2022-23 puts India's annual post-harvest food loss at 74 million tonnes (~22% of foodgrain output, ~10% of total foodgrain and horticulture production combined). NABCONS estimates the economic value at USD 17,703 million per year (~₹1.5 lakh crore). Source: ICAR, NABCONS 2020-2022.

🌍 The 3.3 Billion Tonne Insight

Numbers two and three together: Global plastic production = 1.8 GT CO2e/year. Global food waste = 3.3 GT CO2e/year. Even if every gram of plastic packaging in the world vanished overnight, we would still have 3.3 GT of food-waste emissions every year — emissions whose primary lever for reduction is, ironically, better packaging.

The 2014 activist version of me would have argued you can fix both — better packaging doesn't have to mean plastic. In some narrow categories (cardboard, glass, refillable containers) that's right. But for the bulk of the food supply that feeds India — perishables that need oxygen and moisture barriers, that need to survive 40°C transit, that need to cost ₹0.30 per gram of packaging because the food itself sells at ₹2 per gram — the alternatives don't exist at scale. Glass triples freight emissions. Tinplate quadruples them. Paper alone fails the moisture barrier test in a monsoon.

⚡ The Killer Insight

Food waste emits almost twice as much CO2 as all plastic production combined. The bigger climate villain is rotting food, and the cheapest tool to prevent it is exactly the material I used to despise.

But here's the second half: this leverage only holds for *protective* polyethylene use — sealed sachets, vacuum pouches, milk pouches, vaccine cold-chain. It collapses when polyethylene is used carelessly — fresh thin bag every kirana visit, three bags from the vegetable vendor when one would do, the "free polythene" culture that treats a precision-engineered material as disposable. Same material, two completely different stories.

🔮 What Would Actually Happen Without Polyethylene

The thought experiment: imagine flexible packaging in India disappears overnight. What replaces it?

Milk. The pouch that delivers a litre of milk to your doorstep weighs 4-5 grams of polyethylene and costs about 30 paise. Without it, milk reverts to brass containers (heavy, contamination risk) or reusable bottles (10× freight emissions per litre). The Mother Dairy supply chain that brings 30 lakh litres of milk into Delhi every day exists because of the polyethylene pouch.

Atta. Wheat flour ships in 5-kg laminate bags that protect against insects and moisture for 6+ months. The traditional alternative — cloth bags — has documented insect contamination losses of 10-30% in Indian climate, plus rodent damage. The laminate bag loses essentially zero. Cost of laminate per kg of atta: less than ₹2.

Snacks and pharma. The 30-gram pouch of Lay's that costs ₹10 retail has perhaps ₹0.30 worth of metallised film around it. Without that film, the products either don't exist or cost 5× as much. Same for vaccine cold-chain pouches — the COVID-19 vaccine roll-out in India in 2021 was logistically possible only because of multilayer pouches.

This is not abstract. The countries with the lowest packaging-per-capita also have the highest food spoilage rates and the highest infant mortality from foodborne illness. Flexible packaging is part of the development trajectory that pulled a billion Indians out of food insecurity over the past 50 years.

🚨 The Real Villain — Free Polythene Culture

Everything I've argued so far is about polyethylene used as designed: protective barrier, deployed once per shipment, performing measurable food-saving work. That is roughly half the polyethylene flowing through India each day. The other half is what my 22-year-old self was actually angry about — and on reflection, he was right.

Walk into any kirana shop. Buy 200g dhania, 500g tomatoes, biscuits, milk. The vendor gives you four separate thin polythene bags — one per item — and a fifth to carry all four. Five fresh bags, generated in 90 seconds, used for a single trip, almost certainly thrown into household garbage within 24 hours. None is doing protective work. They are doing convenience work — and the cost of that convenience is exactly the disposal disaster filling our drains, rivers, and roadside undergrowth.

This is "free polythene culture" — the cultural assumption, baked into Indian retail behaviour, that a polythene bag is a disposable commodity owed to the customer with every purchase. The bag is not free. The "free" is a price hidden in environmental cost rather than monetary cost. Because it is hidden, no one optimises against it.

The contrast with the *industry* is stark. A snack company negotiates film thickness to the nearest micron. Every gram is justified by a barrier requirement. That precision evaporates the moment the snack reaches the kirana counter — where the carefully-engineered 1.5g pouch gets dropped into a 7g fresh thin bag, then another 7g bag. The industry's discipline meets the consumer's carelessness, and the consumer wins by 10× in plastic mass.

⚡ The Honest Self-Assessment

My 22-year-old self was right about the bags caught in undergrowth on those Uttarakhand trekking trails. Those weren't protective polyethylene doing food-security work. Those were free-polythene-culture bags — generated thoughtlessly, used once, discarded carelessly. The anger was correct. The target was wrong. The villain isn't polyethylene. The villain is treating polyethylene as if it were free.

✅ How to Use Polyethylene Well — A Personal Checklist

This is what I do now. Not "what you should do" — what I have actually changed in my own behaviour over the last several years. If even a fraction of urban Indians did the same, the post-use plastic burden would drop by 30-40% with no industrial change required.

What I Carry / What I Do:

  1. A thick reusable bag in my office sleeve and another in the car. Cost ₹15 once. Has replaced ~800 fresh polythene bags over three years.
  2. I refuse the fresh polythene at the kirana, every time. The first few weeks the shopkeeper looked at me strangely. Now he hands my reusable bag back without comment. The cultural change starts at the counter.
  3. If I do accept a thin bag, I save it and reuse it. A thin bag has a useful life of 4-6 trips if you take care of it. Most consumers use it for 1.
  4. I consolidate purchases. Five items in one bag = 1 bag of plastic. Five items in five bags = 5 bags of plastic. Vendor's default is 5; a polite request changes it to 1.
  5. I keep multilayer snack pouches as garbage liners. A second life for already-deployed protective polyethylene is essentially free environmental gain.
  6. I do NOT refuse protective packaging on milk, atta, snacks, pharma, or vaccines. That packaging is doing the leverage work the calculator measures. Refusing it would create more waste, not less. The category I refuse is fresh single-use carry bags, not engineered protective films.
  7. Online orders: I request "no extra wrapping" in delivery notes. Half the time it works. Even half-time adoption across millions of orders is meaningful.

None of these are heroic. They cost almost nothing. They distinguish protective polyethylene (worth keeping) from careless polyethylene (worth refusing). The distinction matters because lumping them together leads to bad policy — banning all flexible packaging would crash food security; doing nothing about free-polythene culture continues the disposal disaster.

⚖️ The Honest Other Side

None of the above defends mismanaged plastic disposal. India generates roughly 4 million tonnes of plastic waste per year. Less than 30% is properly recycled. The rest goes to landfill, gets incinerated in the open, or washes into rivers. Multi-layer plastic — the very category most metallised structures fall into — is the hardest to recycle and most environmentally problematic post-use.

The honest position: the *production-and-use* side of plastic packaging is a net positive for food security and climate, while the *post-use* side is a real and unsolved problem the industry needs to fix. The April 2026 EPR amendment and the global shift to mono-material structures (covered in Blog #31) are the corrective forces. They are imperfect. They are happening.

💭 What Changed in My Head

I am still uncomfortable about plastic in oceans. I still want to live in a country with a working recycling system. None of that has changed in nine years.

What changed is where I now think the villain sits. I used to think it was the material — polyethylene itself. Nine years of working with it has convinced me the villain is more specific and more fixable: the cultural pattern of treating polyethylene as if it were free. The free polythene at the kirana counter. The third bag the vendor hands you when one would do. The single-use mindset applied to a material engineered for protection. The polymer itself, deployed thoughtfully — sealed sachet, vacuum pouch, milk bag, vaccine carrier — is one of the most leveraged interventions humans have invented.

The 22-year-old who walked the hills of Uttarakhand and felt anger at the wrappers caught in undergrowth was not wrong to feel anger. The wrapper in the undergrowth was a free-polythene-culture failure, not a packaging-industry failure. The wrapper that kept the snack edible for six months and shipped it to a village 2,000 km away — that wrapper was the industry doing what the industry is supposed to do. Both are similar polymers. Only one was used as designed. Telling them apart is the entire moral argument.

I work in this industry now. I am proud of the work because the protective side is the side I help engineer. The careless side I refuse in my own daily choices — cloth bag, no fresh polythene, distinguishing protective from disposable. Industry has work to do. Disposal infrastructure has work to do. Consumer culture has work to do. All three, simultaneously, are true.

Context के लिए: 190 million Indians undernourished हैं। हर credible study में cited chief reasons — inadequate cold chain, poor storage, fragmented logistics — वो problems हैं जहाँ flexible packaging solution का part है।

🌍 3.3 Billion Tonne Insight

Numbers दो और तीन साथ: Global plastic production = 1.8 GT CO2e/year। Global food waste = 3.3 GT CO2e/year। अगर world में हर gram plastic packaging रात भर में गायब हो जाए, हमारे पास अब भी हर साल 3.3 GT food-waste emissions होंगे — जिनके reduction का primary lever, ironically, better packaging है।

2014 का activist Vivek argue करता कि आप दोनों fix कर सकते हैं — better packaging का मतलब plastic packaging होना ज़रूरी नहीं है। कुछ narrow categories में (cardboard, glass, refillable containers) वो सही है। पर food supply के bulk के लिए जो India को feed करती है — perishables जिन्हें oxygen और moisture barriers चाहिए, जिन्हें 40°C transit survive करना है, जिन्हें ₹0.30 per gram packaging cost पर रहना है क्योंकि food खुद ₹2 per gram पर बिकता है — alternatives scale पर exist नहीं करते। Glass freight emissions triple करता है। Tinplate quadruple। Paper monsoon में moisture barrier test fail करता है।

⚡ Killer Insight

Food waste सारी plastic production combined से लगभग दोगुनी CO2 emit करती है। बड़ा climate villain rotting food है, और उसे रोकने का सबसे सस्ता tool exactly वो material है जिससे मैं nafrat करता था।

पर यहाँ दूसरा half है: ये leverage सिर्फ़ *protective* polyethylene use के लिए hold होता है — sealed sachets, vacuum pouches, milk pouches, vaccine cold-chain। Collapse हो जाता है जब polyethylene careless use होता है — हर kirana visit पर fresh thin bag, vegetable vendor से तीन bags जब एक काफ़ी होती, "free polythene" culture जो precision-engineered material को disposable treat करती है। Same material, दो completely different stories।

🔮 Polyethylene के बिना Actually क्या होगा

Thought experiment: imagine करें India में flexible packaging रात भर में disappear हो जाए। क्या replace करता है?

दूध। आपके doorstep तक litre दूध deliver करने वाली pouch 4-5g polyethylene weight करती है और लगभग 30 paise cost करती है। उसके बिना, दूध brass containers (heavy, contamination risk) या reusable bottles (10× freight emissions per litre) पर revert करता है। Mother Dairy supply chain जो रोज़ Delhi में 30 lakh litres दूध लाती है polyethylene pouch की वजह से exist करती है।

आटा। गेहूँ का आटा 5-kg laminate bags में ship होता है जो 6+ months के लिए insects और moisture से protect करते हैं। Traditional alternative — cloth bags — Indian climate में documented insect contamination losses 10-30% रखते हैं। Laminate bag essentially zero खोता है। Per kg laminate cost ₹2 से कम है।

Snacks और pharma। Lay's की 30g pouch जो ₹10 retail पर बिकती है उसके around लगभग ₹0.30 worth metallised film है। उस film के बिना, products या तो exist नहीं करते या 5× ज़्यादा cost करते। Same vaccine cold-chain pouches के लिए — India में 2021 का COVID vaccine roll-out logistically सिर्फ़ multilayer pouches की वजह से possible था।

ये abstract नहीं है। जिन countries में सबसे कम packaging-per-capita है उनमें सबसे ज़्यादा food spoilage rates और foodborne illness से सबसे ज़्यादा infant mortality है। Flexible packaging उस development trajectory का part है जिसने पिछले 50 साल में एक billion Indians को food insecurity से बाहर निकाला।

🚨 असली Villain — Free Polythene Culture

अब तक मैंने जो argue किया वो polyethylene के बारे में है जैसा वो designed था: protective barrier, हर shipment पर एक बार deployed, measurable food-saving काम करते हुए। ये लगभग आधी polyethylene है जो India में हर रोज़ flow करती है। बाक़ी आधी वो है जिस पर मेरा 22 साल का self actually गुस्सा था — और reflection पर, वो सही था।

किसी भी kirana shop में जाएँ। 200g धनिया, 500g टमाटर, biscuits, दूध खरीदें। Vendor आपको चार अलग thin polythene bags देगा — हर item के लिए एक — और पाँचवीं सब को carry करने के लिए। पाँच fresh bags, 90 seconds में generate हुईं, एक trip के लिए use हुईं, almost ज़रूर household garbage में 24 hours में डाल दी गईं। कोई protective काम नहीं कर रही। वो convenience काम कर रही हैं — और उसकी cost exactly वो disposal disaster है जो हमारे drains, rivers, और roadside undergrowth भर देती है।

ये है "free polythene culture" — Indian retail behaviour में baked cultural assumption कि polythene bag एक disposable commodity है जो हर purchase के साथ customer को owed है। Bag free नहीं है। "Free" एक hidden price है monetary cost की जगह environmental cost में। क्योंकि वो hidden है, कोई उसके against optimise नहीं करता।

*Industry* से contrast stark है। Snack company film thickness को nearest micron पर negotiate करती है। हर gram specific barrier requirement से justify होता है। वो precision moment evaporate हो जाती है जब snack kirana counter पर पहुँचता है — जहाँ carefully-engineered 1.5g pouch एक 7g fresh thin bag में drop हो जाता है, फिर दूसरी 7g bag में। Industry की discipline consumer की carelessness से मिलती है, और consumer plastic mass में 10× factor से जीतता है।

⚡ Honest Self-Assessment

मेरा 22 साल का self Uttarakhand trekking trails पर undergrowth में फँसी जिन bags को देखकर सही था। वो protective polyethylene नहीं थीं food-security काम करती हुईं। वो free-polythene-culture bags थीं — thoughtlessly generate हुईं, एक बार use हुईं, carelessly discard हुईं। Anger correct था। Target ग़लत था। Villain polyethylene नहीं है। Villain polyethylene को free treat करना है।

✅ Polyethylene को सही तरह कैसे Use करें — Personal Checklist

ये वो है जो मैं अब करता हूँ। "आपको क्या करना चाहिए" नहीं — मैंने पिछले कई सालों में अपने behaviour में actually क्या बदला। अगर urban Indians का एक fraction भी यही करे, post-use plastic burden 30-40% गिर जाएगा बिना किसी industrial change के।

मैं क्या Carry करता हूँ / क्या करता हूँ:

  1. एक थिक reusable bag office sleeve में और एक car में। ₹15 की एक बार cost। तीन साल में ~800 fresh polythene bags replace कर दीं।
  2. हर बार kirana पर fresh polythene refuse करता हूँ। पहले कुछ हफ़्तों में shopkeeper strangely देखता था। अब बिना comment किए reusable bag वापस देता है। Cultural change counter पर शुरू होती है।
  3. अगर thin bag accept करता हूँ, उसे save और reuse करता हूँ। Thin bag की useful life 4-6 trips है अगर ध्यान रखें। ज़्यादातर consumers 1 trip use करते हैं।
  4. Purchases consolidate करता हूँ। एक bag में पाँच items = 1 bag plastic। पाँच bags में पाँच items = 5 bags plastic। Vendor की default 5 है; polite request 1 कर देती है।
  5. Multilayer snack pouches को garbage liners की तरह रखता हूँ। पहले से deployed protective polyethylene का दूसरा life essentially free environmental gain है।
  6. दूध, आटा, snacks, pharma, vaccines की protective packaging refuse नहीं करता। वो packaging calculator measure वाला leverage काम कर रही है। Refuse करना ज़्यादा waste create करेगा। मैं fresh single-use carry bags refuse करता हूँ, engineered protective films नहीं।
  7. Online orders: "no extra wrapping" delivery notes में request करता हूँ। आधे time काम करता है। Millions of orders पर आधा-time adoption भी meaningful है।

कोई heroic नहीं है। ये protective polyethylene (worth keeping) को careless polyethylene (worth refusing) से distinguish करते हैं। Distinction matter करता है क्योंकि उन्हें lump करना bad policy की तरफ़ ले जाता है — सारी flexible packaging ban करना food security crash करेगा; free-polythene culture के बारे में कुछ न करना disposal disaster जारी रखेगा।

⚖️ Honest दूसरी Side

ऊपर कुछ भी mismanaged plastic disposal का defense नहीं है। India हर साल लगभग 4 million tonnes plastic waste generate करता है। 30% से कम properly recycle होता है। बाक़ी landfill जाता है, खुले में incinerate होता है, या rivers में wash होता है। Multi-layer plastic — वही category जिसमें ज़्यादातर metallised structures आती हैं — सबसे hard recycle करने और post-use सबसे problematic है।

Honest position: plastic packaging की *production-and-use* side food security और climate के लिए net positive है, जबकि *post-use* side real और unsolved problem है जिसे industry को fix करना है। April 2026 EPR amendment और global mono-material structures shift (Blog #31) corrective forces हैं। वो imperfect हैं। वो हो रही हैं।

💭 मेरे मन में क्या बदला

मैं अब भी समुद्र में plastic के बारे में uncomfortable हूँ। मैं अब भी एक working recycling system वाले देश में रहना चाहता हूँ। नौ साल में इसमें से कुछ नहीं बदला।

जो बदला वो है कि villain अब कहाँ बैठता है। मैं सोचता था villain material है — polyethylene itself। नौ साल इसके साथ काम करने ने convince किया कि villain ज़्यादा specific और ज़्यादा fixable है: polyethylene को free treat करने का cultural pattern। Kirana counter पर free polythene। Vendor की तीसरी bag जब एक काफ़ी होती। Single-use mindset जो protection के लिए engineered material पर apply होती है। Polymer itself, thoughtfully deployed — sealed sachet, vacuum pouch, milk bag, vaccine carrier — humans द्वारा invented most leveraged interventions में से एक है।

22 साल का Vivek जो Uttarakhand की पहाड़ियों पर चला और undergrowth में फँसे wrappers पर anger feel की anger feel करने में ग़लत नहीं था। Undergrowth में wrapper free-polythene-culture failure थी, packaging-industry failure नहीं। वो wrapper जिसने snack छह महीने तक edible रखा और 2,000 km दूर एक गाँव तक ship किया — वो industry वो काम कर रही थी जो industry को करना चाहिए। दोनों similar polymers हैं। सिर्फ़ एक designed तरह use हुआ। उन्हें apart बताना पूरा moral argument है।

मैं अब इस industry में काम करता हूँ। मुझे काम पर proud है क्योंकि protective side वो side है जिसे मैं engineer करने में help करता हूँ। Careless side मैं अपनी daily choices में refuse करता हूँ — cloth bag, no fresh polythene, protective को disposable से distinguish करना। Industry का काम है। Disposal infrastructure का काम है। Consumer culture का काम है। तीनों, simultaneously, सच हैं।

💡 Key Takeaway: Polyethylene villain नहीं है। Villain वो cultural pattern है जो polyethylene को free treat करती है — हर kirana visit पर fresh thin bags, multiple bags जब एक काफ़ी होती, assumption जो precision-engineered material को convenience काम पर waste करती है। वही polymer, sealed sachet या vacuum pouch के रूप में thoughtfully deployed, Earth पर highest-leverage food-security interventions में से एक है। Plastic CO2 globally: 1.8 GT/year। Food waste CO2 globally: 3.3 GT/year। Cucumber wrap leverage: 167:1। India का annual food loss: 74 MT, ₹1.5 lakh crore। Protective side credit deserve करती है जो rarely मिलती है। Careless side criticism deserve करती है जो हमेशा मिली है। उन्हें apart बताना पूरा moral argument है।
