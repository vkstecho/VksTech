🚨 Operator Emergency Response — 3 Steps in 2 Minutes

Three AM. The tension alarm goes off. You look through the viewing window — the web has snapped. Don't panic and don't start adjusting parameters yet. Every web break follows the same three-step recovery. These are not optional or situational. They are mandatory in this exact order.

Step 1 — Stop Everything (10 seconds)

Hit the E-Stop. Close the vent valve. Engage the brake on both unwinder and rewinder. The goal is to freeze the machine state exactly as the break happened — this preserves evidence for diagnosis.

Step 2 — Photograph the Tear (30 seconds)

Before you touch anything, take phone photos of both torn edges — unwinder side and rewinder side. The shape of the tear tells the supervisor what caused the break. Moving or cleaning the film first destroys this evidence.

Step 3 — Log the Break Before Re-threading (60 seconds)

Open Format F/16 (Web Break Record) and fill four fields: time, jumbo number, tear location, machine speed at moment of break. This takes less than a minute and is the single most important habit separating plants that eliminate web breaks from plants that keep having them.

👀 Tear Pattern Quick Reference

The shape of the tear tells you which component failed. Before the supervisor arrives, match the tear to one of these five patterns — it saves 20 minutes of diagnostic back-and-forth.

Tear PatternWhat It MeansFirst Fix to Try
Clean straight edge (CD)Sudden tension spike — usually wrinkle formation that folded and brokeCheck tension log for pulses, inspect dancer roller
Diagonal tearOff-axis tension — one side of web is pulling harder than the otherVerify roller alignment upstream; check bearing play
Ragged irregular tearEdge damage propagated inward under tensionInspect last 500m of unwinding roll for nicks or knife marks
Pinhole cluster tearPre-existing pinhole defect under stress concentrationThis is a pre-existing film defect — raise NCR with supplier
Long fibrous tear along MDExcessive tension during acceleration or decelerationReview tension profile during startup and shutdown cycles

💡 One warning — Never re-thread and restart without supervisor approval

If the same tear pattern repeats after restart, the root cause is still there. Continuing to run will produce more breaks, more waste, and mask the actual problem. A 15-minute diagnostic pause saves 6 hours of downtime later.

🛡️ Prevention Checklist — 6 Weekly Supervisor Habits

  • Monday morning: review the F/16 log from the past week. Same tear pattern twice in a week means systemic cause, not random event. Open an investigation.
  • Bearing play inspection on all tension-critical rollers every PM. Push each laterally by hand. Any felt play means replace immediately — no exceptions, no next-week scheduling.
  • Laser alignment check monthly across the tension control zone (dancer, tension roller, drum approach). Floors settle over time; alignment drifts.
  • Calibrate tension sensors quarterly against a physical reference gauge. Displayed tension is only as accurate as the last calibration.
  • Walk the machine during production once per shift. Listen for noise changes at bearings. Feel idlers for vibration. Early warning beats reactive repair.
  • Film quality NCR only after ruling out causes 1-6. Cause #7 being misdiagnosed as cause #1 damages the supplier relationship and masks real plant-side problems.

🏭 Real Shop Floor Story — The ₹21 Lakh Bearing

A production metalliser plant was averaging 3-4 web breaks per week on Met-2. Every break was dismissed as "film quality" and logged as supplier fault. Over six months, accumulated downtime, waste, and B-Grade rolls had reached a level that triggered the finance team to ask questions.

A senior technical consultant was brought in. His first request was unusual: "Show me your F/16 records for the last 90 days."

The plant had the records. They were mostly blank — only date, jumbo number, and "film quality" written in the cause column. No tear photos. No machine speed at break. No tension reading. The record existed for audit compliance, not for diagnosis.

The consultant asked the next shift to log properly — tear photo, exact tension, exact speed, exact roller positions. After 6 days of proper logging, the pattern was obvious: every break occurred during a specific acceleration ramp, and every tear photo showed the same diagonal signature.

That narrowed it to one cause category: tension pulse during acceleration. From the 8-cause ranking, that pointed to causes #1 (bearing play), #4 (acceleration spike), or #6 (PLC noise).

They pulled the dancer roller bearings that weekend. One bearing — the one feeding tension data to the PLC — had developed a 0.3mm radial play. Not enough to make noise. Not enough to show up on visual PM check. But enough to send wrong tension signals during acceleration, which caused the PLC to over-correct, which created the fluctuation, which led to the diagonal tear.

One ₹4,200 bearing was costing the plant ₹21 lakh per month in downtime, waste, and B-Grade downgrades. Six days of proper F/16 logging found it. The plant now runs F/16 religiously. Monthly web break rate dropped from 14 to 2.
